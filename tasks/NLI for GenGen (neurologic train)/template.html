Thanks for participating in this HIT! Please read the instructions and examples below, then complete the HIT (5 questions).

In this HIT, you will be asked to label the relationship between statements (called Natural Language Inference).

For each question, you will be given 2 sentences:

PremiseSentence describing a real-world situation, always assumed to be true.
Hypothesis A hypothesis assumption or inference that we might make about the situation having read the premise.

For each question, your goal is to select the best Relationship between the two statements:

Entails The hypothesis can definitely be assumed CORRECT given the premise. If premise is true, hypothesis MUST also be true .
Example: If we assume premise Typically birds fly long distances. to be true, then the hypothesis Typically birds fly must also be true.
Contradicts The hypothesis can definitely be assumed WRONG given the premise. If the premise is true, hypothesis MUST be false.
Example: If we assume premise Typically birds fly. to be true, then the hypothesis Birds can only walk must be false.
Neutral The hypothesis might be true given premise, but the relationship isn't direct. For example, both the premise and the hypothesis can be true for differing reasons. More context is needed to determine the two statement's relationship.
Example: Assuming the truth of premise Typically birds fly. cannot help us decide if the hypothesis Typically birds eat bugs. is true or false. Birds eating bugs is not directly related to their ability to fly. The fact both statements are correct is a coincidence.
HUH?! Cannot judge. The hypothesis and/or the premise does not make sense; OR I don't know enough about what the statements are saying to judge.

RULE of THUMB:

  • Please assume that the premise is true, THEN decide its relationship to the hypothesis. (See Example 5 below)
  • If you can't understand what either of the statement is saying, you have a HUH?! siutation.
  • If the two statements basically say the same thing, then you know you have a Entails relationship.
  • If the two statements cannot co-exist in the real world, then you know you have a Contradicts relationship.
  • If your answer is "depends", then you likely have a Neutral relationship.
EX #1
Premise: A man sits on a couch reading a book.
Hypothesis: The man is asleep.
Contradicts
If it is true that the man is reading a book, the man cannot be sleeping at the same time.
EX #2
Premise: Children smiling and waving at camera.
Hypothesis: They are smiling at their parents
Neutral
It is possible, but cannot be concluded without more information. The conclusion would depend on who is behind the camera, for example.
EX #3
Premise: A boy is jumping on skateboard in the middle of a red bridge.
Hypothesis: The boy does a skateboarding trick.
Entails
Jumping on a skateboard is a skateboarding trick.
EX #4
Premise: Typically, a bicycle has two wheels.
Hypothesis: Typically, a bicycle has wheels.
Entails
If there are two wheels, you can safely assume that there are wheels, in the first place.
EX #5
Premise: Typically, a bicycle has four wheels.
Hypothesis: Typically, a bicycle an odd number of wheels.
Contradicts
If there are four wheels, you have an even (not odd!) number of wheels. Note, we aren't asking you to judge the truthfulness of the statements.
EX #6
Premise: Typically, a bicycle has two wheels.
Hypothesis: Bicycles can come in road, mountain, or cruiser varieties.
Neutral
Both statements are reasonable statements about bikes, but they have no relationship to each other. Varieties of bicycles cannot be derived simply by knowing how many wheels it has. The hypothesis doesn't contradict the premise. And the premise doesn't entail the premise either: hypothesis can be true even if the premise is false (a broken bike with missing wheels, say).
EX #8
Premise: Typically, fructose is found in fruit juice.
Hypothesis: Typically, fructose is contained in fruit juice.
Entails
The two statements say the exact same thing in two ways.
EX #9
Premise: Wildcats are known for their agressive behavior.
Hypothesis: Typically, a wildcat, or wild cat.
HUH?!
It is not entirely clear what the hypothesis is saying, so the judgement cannot be made.
